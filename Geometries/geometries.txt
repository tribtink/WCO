A Minimal Formal Framework for Coordination Systems as High‑Dimensional Geometries
1. Introduction
Coordination systems—ranging from interpersonal cooperation to interstate conflict—are typically described using taxonomies: layers, stages, or categories such as “peace,” “war,” “governance,” or “strategy.” These taxonomies fail to capture the fluidity of real systems, where concepts such as trust, legitimacy, conflict, and power do not remain confined to single layers.

This paper proposes a shift from taxonomy to geometry.
Instead of classifying coordination phenomena into discrete tiers, we model them as points and trajectories in a multi‑axis state space. Peace and war are not separate systems; they are regions within a shared geometry.

This framework is derived from a minimal ontology (Tier‑0 primitives) and generates the structural forms (Tier‑1 composites) that appear across political science, organizational theory, cybernetics, and conflict studies.

2. Tier‑0 Ontology: Minimal Primitives
We define the following primitives as necessary and sufficient for any coordination system:

Reality — the domain of states and transformations.

Information — signals available to agents.

Epistemics — processes for interpreting information.

Agency — loci of choice.

Power — differential capacity to transform states.

Incentives — objective functions shaping action.

Trust — expectations about reliability of others.

Conflict Containment — mechanisms limiting destructive interaction.

Transformation — allowable operations on states.

Objective Function — system‑level or agent‑level goals.

These primitives are invariants: they exist in all coordination systems regardless of scale, context, or time horizon.

5.3 Cascades
Peace → war transitions occur when:

epistemic quality falls

trust decays

containment weakens

grievances accumulate

power asymmetry rises

context crosses a threshold

War → peace transitions require:

external containment

security guarantees

epistemic repair

trust rebuilding

grievance transformation

These transitions are asymmetric (hysteresis).

7. Implications
This framework:

unifies peace and war as regions of a shared system

explains why concepts like trust, legitimacy, and conflict resist tiered classification

provides a generative basis for institutions, markets, hierarchies, and epistemic systems

enables modeling of transitions, cascades, and regime shifts

supports simulation, prediction, and intervention design

It is not a taxonomy but a coordinate system for coordination.

8. Conclusion
Coordination systems are best understood not as layered taxonomies but as geometries. A minimal ontology generates the structures observed across social, political, and organizational domains. Transition dynamics reveal how systems move between cooperative and adversarial regimes. This geometric framework provides a foundation for a general coordination substrate capable of modeling peace, conflict, governance, adaptation, and evolution.
