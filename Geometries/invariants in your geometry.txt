What “invariants” means in your geometry
In this substrate, invariants are the things that stay structurally true across:

peace ↔ war

cooperation ↔ adversariality

individual ↔ civilization

They’re not constants in the sense of “never changing values”; they’re conserved structures and constraints—the grammar of possible states and transitions.

From your own text:

“War and peace are not different systems.
They are different regions of the same system’s state–context space.”

The invariants are what make it “the same system.”

1. Ontological invariants
These are the “always present” primitives—if any of these are missing, you don’t have a coordination system at all.

Agents:  
There is always at least one locus of decision and action (individuals, groups, institutions, AIs).
No agents → no coordination, no conflict.

Resources and constraints:  
Scarcity, limits, or trade‑offs always exist (time, attention, energy, material, bandwidth, legitimacy).
Without constraints, there is no real choice, hence no strategy.

Information:  
Signals, observations, messages, or data are always present, even if degraded or manipulated.
No information → no epistemics, no learning, no deception.

These three—agents, resources, information—are ontological invariants.

2. Structural invariants
These are properties of how things are wired, regardless of whether the system is in peace or war.

Topology of interaction:  
There is always some graph: who can affect whom, who can observe whom, who can coordinate with whom.
Edges can change, but “there is a graph” is invariant.

Role structure:  
There are always roles (even if informal): initiators, responders, mediators, spoilers, stewards, free‑riders.
Labels change, but the functional roles recur.

Channels and interfaces:  
There are always pathways for influence (markets, laws, norms, APIs, protocols, media, violence).
The specific channel mix changes; the existence of channels does not.

These are structural invariants: the system is always “some graph of roles over channels.”

3. Epistemic invariants
These are about knowledge and belief—what is always true about how agents know and don’t know.

Partial information:  
No agent has complete, perfectly accurate knowledge of the full state.
Uncertainty is invariant; only its degree and distribution vary.

Asymmetry:  
Some agents know more than others, or know different things.
This asymmetry is what makes deception, signaling, and intelligence meaningful.

Update dynamics:  
Beliefs are always being updated (or failing to update) in response to signals.
Whether via Bayesian reasoning, heuristics, propaganda, or myth, there is always an epistemic process.

These are epistemic invariants: uncertainty, asymmetry, and updating never disappear.

4. Power and agency invariants
These capture the fact that influence is never flat.

Differential capacity to affect outcomes:  
Some agents can move more of the state space than others (resources, authority, coercive means, narrative reach).
Power distribution can shift, but the existence of differential power is invariant.

Agency under constraint:  
Agents always act under constraints (rules, norms, capabilities, risks), but they still have choice.
Zero agency → no strategy; zero constraint → no meaningful strategy.

Feedback from action to capacity:  
Actions change future power (investments, reputations, alliances, deterrence).
Power is both a state and a function of history—this feedback loop is invariant.

These are power/agency invariants: there is always uneven, history‑dependent capacity to act.

5. Incentive invariants
These are about why agents move at all.

Objective functions (local):  
Every agent has some implicit or explicit objective (survival, profit, status, ideology, stability, growth).
The content varies; the existence of objectives does not.

Externalities:  
Actions by one agent affect others, often in ways not fully priced or internalized.
Without externalities, there is no conflict of interest, no commons problems.

Misalignment potential:  
Local objectives can diverge from system‑level health or global objectives.
This misalignment potential is invariant; only its severity changes.

These are incentive invariants: agents always optimize something, and those optimizations always interact.

6. Trust and legitimacy invariants
These govern whether coordination is cheap or expensive.

Reliance on expectations:  
Coordination always depends on expectations about others’ behavior (promises, norms, reputations, institutions).
Even in war, strategy assumes some regularity.

Vulnerability to betrayal:  
Trust always creates exposure; betrayal is always possible.
If betrayal were impossible, trust wouldn’t be a meaningful variable.

Legitimacy as a meta‑resource:  
Systems always rely on some story of “rightful” authority or procedure (law, tradition, consent, performance).
The narrative changes; the need for legitimacy does not.

These are trust/legitimacy invariants: expectation, vulnerability, and justification are always in play.

7. Conflict containment and transformation invariants
These define how conflict exists without instantly destroying the system.

From your text:

“War is not primarily about weapons. Weapons are one implementation. At a deeper level, war is organized competition between groups pursuing objectives under conditions of limited trust.”

Conflict is ever‑present:  
Interests are never perfectly aligned; conflict is always latent or active.

Containment mechanisms:  
There are always mechanisms that shape how conflict expresses (law, norms, markets, rituals, courts, elections, taboos, deterrence, violence).
The specific mechanisms change; the existence of some containment is invariant—otherwise the system collapses immediately.

Transformability:  
Conflicts can be transformed (reframed, traded, compensated, institutionalized, sublimated).
The possibility of transformation is invariant; only its accessibility and cost vary.

These are conflict invariants: conflict is constant; only its form and containment differ.

8. System‑level objective invariants
Finally, there are invariants at the level of the whole substrate.

Persistence vs. collapse:  
Every coordination system is implicitly “trying” to persist as a recognizable pattern rather than dissolve.
If persistence were not a pressure, there would be no system to model.

Throughput of value/information:  
Systems must maintain some minimum throughput (material, informational, or symbolic) to remain viable.
The channels change; the need for throughput does not.

Adaptation pressure:  
Environments change; systems that cannot adapt eventually fail.
Adaptation pressure is invariant; only the tempo and intensity vary.

These are system‑objective invariants: persistence, throughput, and adaptation are always in the background.

How this plugs back into your geometry
In your “taxonomy → geometry” move, the invariants are:

the fixed grammar of the state space

the constraints on trajectories through that space

Axes (peace/war, cooperative/adversarial, individual/civilizational, etc.) describe where you are.
Invariants describe what must always be true no matter where you are.
